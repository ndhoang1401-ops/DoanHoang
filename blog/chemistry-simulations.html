Chemistry Meets Software:
Interactive Simulations

Mar 1, 2024 · 7 min read · Nguyễn Doãn Hoàng
Three.js WebGL Chemistry Education Simulation

One of my favourite projects has been building interactive chemistry simulations — software that helps people see what's impossible to see with the naked eye. Electrons filling orbitals. Particles colliding with thermal velocity. Proteins folding into structure. This is where code stops being a tool and starts being a microscope.

Why Chemistry & Software Belong Together

Chemistry is the foundation of almost every major technology problem of our time. But it's almost invisible in tech education. That's a gap worth closing:

  • Battery technology: understanding lithium-ion chemistry at the atomic level directly leads to better devices and faster charging
  • Drug discovery: computational chemistry has compressed decade-long research timelines into months
  • Materials science: new materials — stronger, lighter, more conductive — emerge from understanding how atoms interact
  • Climate tech: carbon capture, hydrogen fuel cells, next-gen solar — all built on chemistry

The problem: traditional chemistry education is frozen in time. Students memorise formulas without ever building intuition for why atoms bond the way they do. Software changes that.

The Power of Seeing in 3D

Most people — including most chemistry students — cannot visualise molecular structures in three dimensions. We lack the intuition because we've never seen it. That's exactly the gap software can close.

A few hundred lines of Three.js is enough to show an electron probability cloud rotating in real time. Students who spent months struggling with abstract orbital theory suddenly understand it in minutes when they can grab it, rotate it, and watch electrons fill from the lowest energy state upward.

The feedback I've seen from people using these simulations is consistent: "I finally get it." Not because the content changed — because the medium did.

Building a Chemistry Simulation: The Approach

Physical Accuracy First

Model the real physics. Coulomb's law for electron–nucleus interactions. Angular momentum. Quantum uncertainty where it matters. Libraries like three.js and babylon.js handle rendering — your job is implementing the physics on top.

Interactivity Is the Point

A simulation the user can only watch is just a video. The goal is manipulation: adjust temperature, change atomic number, trigger a reaction, apply a mutation. Passive observation produces passive learning. Interactive systems produce intuition.

GPU or Bust

Simulating thousands of particles at 60fps in a browser requires the GPU. Use WebGL shaders for particle physics where possible. Offload heavy computation to WebWorkers to keep the main thread free. Implement level-of-detail (LOD) — not every particle needs full physics when it's off-screen or far from the camera.

Visual Feedback Is Half the Learning

Colour-code by energy level. Show particle trails so paths are visible. Highlight bond formation moments. Good visualisation isn't decoration — it's the difference between data and understanding.

The Challenges That Aren't Obvious

Accuracy vs. Clarity

Full quantum accuracy makes things unintuitively complex. Full simplification makes them wrong. The sweet spot is: accurate enough to teach real concepts, simple enough to remain interactive and readable. Finding that balance for each simulation is a design problem as much as a technical one.

Scientific Validation

It is very easy to build something visually compelling that is scientifically incorrect. The electron doesn't move in a circle. Bonds don't look like sticks. Every design decision that prioritises aesthetics over accuracy is a lesson taught wrong to someone. Working with actual chemists for review isn't optional — it's part of the build process.

Performance on Real Devices

A simulation that runs at 60fps on a MacBook may run at 8fps on a mid-range Android phone. Always profile on low-end hardware. Adaptive particle counts, frame-skip on slow devices, and WebP textures are not optimisations — they're requirements for reaching the students who need this most.

The Future of Science Education

This is where it's going — and most of it is already possible with existing technology:

Virtual Labs
AR Chemistry
Collaborative Sims
AI-Adaptive Learning

Students in rural schools with no lab access should have the same ability to explore molecular interactions as students at well-funded universities. That's not idealism — it's what a browser, a GPU, and a decent simulation make possible right now.

Getting Started

01

Learn Three.js or Babylon.js

3D rendering in the browser. Start with the fundamentals: scenes, cameras, meshes, lighting. You don't need WebGL from scratch to build something useful.

02

Study basic physics

Newton's laws and Coulomb's law will take you further than you think. You do not need quantum mechanics to build a compelling first simulation.

03

Find a dataset

PubChem and ChemSpider have free APIs with molecular data. PDB has protein structures. Real data is more motivating than made-up examples.

04

Start smaller than you think

Visualise a single molecule rotating before you simulate a reaction. Render a static orbital before you animate electron transitions. Ship something real.

05

Get a chemist to review it

Find someone who knows the science and ask them to break your simulation. They will. Fix it. Repeat.

Closing Thoughts

The intersection of chemistry and software isn't a niche — it's where some of the most important work of the next decade will happen. The next generation of batteries, drugs, and materials will be built by people who understand both the underlying science and the tools to model it.
